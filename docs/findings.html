The numbers in full

Key findings

Returning players are deployed like known quantities and produce like new signings. Both outcomes are reported with their sample size.

The frame

The study compares returning players against all other new-team signings from 2012 through 2025. A return is a free-agent signing with a team the player left in an earlier season and came back to after playing elsewhere. The frame holds 15 returns against 842 other new-team signings.

Sources: return_events.csv and pooled_findings.md.

Time on ice, raw means

+0.84 vs −0.48

Returners run a positive time-on-ice residual. Other new signings run negative. Measured in this study's frame, 11 returns with a residual against 581 other new signings.

Time on ice, tier-controlled

+1.75 min

Return coefficient after adjusting for tier, position, and age. 95 percent interval 0.28 to 3.21. p = 0.02. A signal at this sample size, not a settled result.

Production, raw means

+0.05 vs −0.01

Points-per-game change for returners against other new signings. 9 returns with a measurable value. Descriptive only.

Production, tier-controlled

Not distinct

Return coefficient +0.08, interval crosses zero, p = 0.15. The production side does not separate returners from other new signings.

The same-team and other-new-team baselines are from the play for contract study. Returners arrive as new signings but are deployed above ordinary re-signings.

What the two outcomes say together

Returners get re-signing-grade minutes and new-signing-grade production. Familiarity moves how a player is used. It does not move what he delivers. Priced by the bench, not earned on the ice.

The result is small-sample and is framed that way. The time-on-ice gap rests on 11 players. The production result rests on 9 and is descriptive only. Read the direction. Do not read proof. The full stress-test is on the audit page.
