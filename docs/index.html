Series entry two of the talent reunion studies

The organization reunion

Do NHL players who return to a former team deliver more than other new signings? The bench and the scoresheet disagree.

Headline

Returning players receive re-signing-grade ice time. Their production does not match it.

Some NHL free agents sign back with a team they used to play for. A team holds private information about a player it signed before. The question is whether that information shows up as value on the ice.

It shows up in deployment, not in production. Teams give returners more time on ice per cap dollar than they give other new signings. On the scoresheet, returners look like ordinary new signings. Familiarity is priced by the bench. It is not delivered on the ice.

Time on ice

+1.75 min

Tier-controlled gap versus other new signings, per game. p = 0.02, measured on 11 returns with a residual. A signal, not a settled result.

Production

Flat

Points change for returners is not distinguishable from other new signings. Descriptive only, 9 returns with a measurable value.

The gap in one line

Returners are deployed like known quantities. They produce like new signings. The distance between those two facts is the finding.

The comparison

How this fits the prior work

The finding sits against three reference points, each from a different source.

The play-for-contract study found same-team re-signings deliver a positive time-on-ice residual and new-team signings a negative one. Returners arrive as new signings but land above ordinary re-signings on deployment.

The NFL Coach Continuity study found on-field coaching familiarity produced no measurable effect on retained production. This study asks the contractual-familiarity version of that question. The answer rhymes. Familiarity moves how a player is used more than what he produces.

The management literature on boomerang employees finds no rehire performance premium and flatter growth over time. Two domains, the same shape.

Scope and limits

This is a small-sample, descriptive study, and it is framed that way on purpose.

The sample is 15 free-agent returns from 2012 through 2025. The time-on-ice result rests on 11 of them. Two or three players could move it. Read it as direction, not proof.

Returns are detected from free-agent signings only. Trade-backs are excluded because the contract data source does not carry trades. The case that prompted this study, Connor Clifton's 2026 return to Boston, came partly through a trade and is not in the analyzed data.

The planned split by whether the general manager or head coach remained was not run. The return count is too small to support it. That decomposition is documented as a future extension if the sample grows.

Method

The study compares returning players against all other new-team signings. A return is a free-agent signing with a team the player left in a prior season and returned to after playing elsewhere.

The outcomes are two. Time on ice residual measures deployment: expected versus actual minutes given pay. Points change measures production before and after the signing. The time-on-ice machinery is reused from the play-for-contract study in this suite.

Player stint history is visible from the 2009-2010 season onward. A small number of returns whose first stint began before then may be truncated.
